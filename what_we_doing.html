New to Yoga? Things to Know Before Taking Your First Class

If you're preparing to attend a yoga class for the first time, it would be perfectly natural to feel some low-level anxiety. You might be worrying: What's it like to execute a Downward Dog pose? How well will I be able to follow the teacher's instructions? Can I fit in with experienced yogis? Knowing what to expect can help you maximize what you get out of the class.

Here are 10 things you should expect in are class:

  • You'll learn new breathing exercises. Everyone breathes to stay alive, but in yoga you`ll be learning breathing practices to do with poses and movement. The breath is thought to be associated with the energy of the body, and we know from science that changes in your breath can change your heart rate and nervous system.

  • Be prepared to move and do a series of poses. The poses you'll do might be seated, standing or lying down. Regardless of what the instructor suggests, you shouldn't do anything that hurts you or feels uncomfortable. Every pose has countless variations, and no one knows your body better than you.

  • The yoga instructor will ask you to pay attention. Store your cellphone in your gym bag or leave it in your locker when you're in yoga class and pay attention to the instructor. Yoga is a mindfulness practice, in which you need to be present in the moment.Part of the practice is to notice what`s happening in your body, what's happening with your senses, what's happening in the world around you.

  • You'll probably feel lost. As with many endeavors you're trying for the first time, you probably won`t know how to do many or even any of the poses the instructor leads you through.You're not expected to know anything at your first class, and that's perfectly fine. That's why the instructor is there. Don't feel bad if you spend a good part of your first class looking at other students to see what pose you're supposed to be doing, it's part of the learning process.

  • You're likely to feel slightly uncomfortable. When we move our bodies in new ways, it always feels a little awkward or uncomfortable. Don't be alarmed if you feel some awkwardness or discomfort that's normal, and every first-time yogi goes through that stage. Just observe and relish being in the beginner's mindset. This allows you to feel and notice things you don`t usually perceive. There's the inherent discomfort of being in a new space with new people doing something new that may encourage a feeling of vulnerability.

  • Props will probably be available. Props can be particularly useful for people with certain chronic conditions, like arthritis, which can make you stiff, or fibromyalgia, which is associated with joint pain. But props can be useful for many people, whether they have a chronic condition or not. For example, if a yoga pose involves reaching forward to grab your feet while sitting, but you don`t have the flexibility to do that, you can use a strap to lengthen your reach.

  • The instructor may offer adjustments. Instructor will offer adjustments while you're engaged in a pose. That means putting their hands on you to guide you. An instructor will typically say that if you don't want to be guided to let him or her know, sometimes by raising a hand. It's really important for students to know they have agency over their own body, and can decline if they don`t want adjustments.

  • Your instructor might lead you in a chant or meditation. At different points in the class, your instructor may lead you in a chant or quiet meditation. This can seem strange if you're not used to it. The chanting is to unify the group, create some sound resonance in the body and shift energy. The meditation calms the mind and improves mental health and cognition. You can choose to try it out or just be in the room and notice how it sounds or feels.

  • The class will probably end with deep relaxation. At the end of many classes, the instructor will ask participants to lay down and, perhaps, to close their eyes. Some teachers will dim the lights and talk the class through a few minutes of guided relaxation, while others will let yogis rest in silence.

  • Expect to feel sore the next day. Practicing yoga is associated with alleviating pain, improved flexibility and toning your muscles. But initially, you may feel soreness. Any time we move in new ways, we use some muscles more intensely than they are used to. If you wake up the morning after your first yoga class with sore thighs or back muscles, that's no cause for alarm.
